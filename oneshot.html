THE QUESTION

What actually runs?

Ship a vision model to the edge and the first question is brutal. Which model runs on which hardware, inside real physical constraints, at which input resolution, and what latency and throughput do you get for it. The lab needed that answer as a table: pick your constraints, read off the optimal config. That table did not exist.

"Everyone benchmarks their lucky config. Nobody benchmarks the grid."

PRIOR ART

Not found.

Published numbers come one lucky config at a time: one model, one device, one resolution, latency only. Nothing proper, nothing in depth. Nothing that put latency, throughput and activation memory side by side on real edge hardware. So the study had to build its own ground truth.

prior art: nothing comprehensive
THE GRID

Combinatorics on purpose.

17 CNNs and Transformers. 8 input resolutions. 4 edge platforms, Jetson Nano and Coral EdgeTPU among them. INT8 throughout, quantized both ways. Every model against every resolution on every device. No cherry-picked runs.

17 × 8 × 4 = 544 configs
THE REAL DIFFICULTY

The harness was the hard part.

Not the models. The engineering around them: every platform brings its own toolchain, every toolchain its own libraries, every library its own version constraints, and physical hardware brings error classes that simulation never shows you. INT8 adds a layer of pain of its own. Keeping 544 configs comparable and reproducible in code, that is the study.

4 toolchains · 1 harness

FORK №1 — PTQ OR QAT?

Both. That's the point.

PTQ alone is the cheap answer. QAT is the industry standard. A study meant to be the reference has to say what QAT's extra training actually buys on the same hardware, so both went into the grid for every config. Rejected: shipping only the easy half.

FORK №2 — WHERE DOES LATENCY LIVE?

The OS is part of the model.

Most benchmarks stop at model compute. This one instrumented activation memory, page-fault rate and base OS time too, because on a small board the operating system's overhead is part of your latency budget. Rejected: pretending the model runs in a vacuum.

latency · throughput · activation mem · page faults
BAKI!! WHAT BROKE

Everything, repeatedly.

Library and hardware compatibility errors, constantly. A version that works on one platform breaks the next. An export that converts cleanly on the desk refuses on the device. Debugging on physical hardware where the only error message is sometimes a power cycle. Every fix got folded back into the harness, which is how the pipeline earned its reproducibility.

THE SCOREBOARD

Against FP32, on the same device.

The baseline is honest: the same model at full FP32 precision on the same hardware. Quantized INT8 came back 35% faster at 90%+ retained accuracy, with the model footprint down 40%. Reproduced across devices, not one run on one board on a good day.

+35% speed · −40% size · 90%+ accuracy · vs FP32
THE OUTPUT

700+ plots. You can't see them.

The study generated over 700 plots and graphs across the grid. They belong to the lab and sit under NDA, so this page describes methods, not findings. The numbers above are the shareable summary; the rest stays in Kharagpur.

EPILOGUE — HINDSIGHT

Redo: the pipeline.

Restarted tomorrow, the grid stays and the pipeline gets rebuilt for efficiency. The first version grew organically, one platform at a time. The rebuild would treat orchestration as the product from day one, so adding model eighteen costs an afternoon, not a week.
